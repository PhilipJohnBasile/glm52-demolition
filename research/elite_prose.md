# Elite Prose: SFT Gold for Healing a Competent-But-Not-Elite Model

This document is a working reference for building supervised fine-tuning (SFT) gold data
targeting elite nonfiction prose. The goal: heal a model that writes competent but flat,
hedged, AI-flavored text into one that writes the way the masters do—clear, tight, specific,
alive.

---

## 1. THE ELITE CANON

Eighteen writers. Each entry: what they perfected and the one principle you steal from them.

### William Strunk Jr. (1869–1946)
*The Elements of Style* (1918, expanded with E.B. White, 1959)

The original surgeon. Every rule serves one end: remove what isn't doing work.

**Signature principle:** "Omit needless words. Vigorous writing is concise. A sentence should
contain no unnecessary words, a paragraph no unnecessary sentences, for the same reason that a
drawing should have no unnecessary lines and a machine no unnecessary parts."

Not *shorter* — *no waste*. A long sentence is fine if every word earns its place.

---

### E.B. White (1899–1985)
*Essays of E.B. White*, *Charlotte's Web*, decades of New Yorker "Notes and Comment"

The craftsman's craftsman. White edited Strunk but surpassed him. His prose is warm where
Strunk is cold, alive where the rules are inert. He wrote about New York and Maine and death
without ever announcing that he was writing about New York, Maine, or death.

**Signature principle:** Specific nouns and active verbs carry prose. Adjectives and adverbs
are the lazy writer's crutch. Use the exact word, not its padded cousin.

"Brevity is not the soul of wit so much as it is the essence of writing."

---

### George Orwell (1903–1950)
*Politics and the English Language* (1946), *Why I Write* (1946)

The moralist of prose. Orwell argued that bad writing and bad politics share a root: the
evasion of clear thought. Jargon, passive constructions, and dead metaphors are not aesthetic
failures—they are ethical ones. They hide what the writer (or the state) is actually doing.

**Six rules (in full):**
1. Never use a metaphor, simile, or other figure of speech you are used to seeing in print.
2. Never use a long word where a short one will do.
3. If it is possible to cut a word out, always cut it out.
4. Never use the passive where you can use the active.
5. Never use a foreign phrase, a scientific word, or a jargon word if you can think of an
   everyday English equivalent.
6. Break any of these rules sooner than say anything outright barbarous.

**Signature principle:** "Good prose is like a windowpane." The goal is transparency—the
reader sees the subject, not the prose.

Orwell demonstrated the principle with a translation exercise. Ecclesiastes: *"I returned and
saw under the sun, that the race is not to the swift, nor the battle to the strong."* Rendered
in modern bureaucratic English: *"Objective consideration of contemporary phenomena compels the
conclusion that success or failure in competitive activities exhibits no tendency to be
commensurate with innate capacity."* Same meaning. One is literature. One is slop.

---

### William Zinsser (1922–2015)
*On Writing Well* (1976, revised seven times)

The teacher. Where Strunk and Orwell are commandments, Zinsser is conversation. His central
argument: clutter is the disease of American writing. Every phrase that serves no function is
clutter—not neutral, actively harmful, because it exhausts the reader before you've said
anything.

**Signature principle:** Simplicity, clarity, brevity, humanity. "Strip every sentence to its
cleanest components." But also: write with your own voice. Authenticity is not a decoration—
it is what makes the prose live.

"The reader is someone with an attention span of about 30 seconds—a person assailed by other
forces competing for time... Your only tools are the 26 letters of the alphabet and whatever
rules of punctuation seem reasonable."

---

### Ernest Hemingway (1899–1961)
*A Farewell to Arms*, *In Our Time*, *The Old Man and the Sea*

The iceberg theorist. Hemingway's best prose contains more in what it omits than in what it
states. He learned economy from journalism—the AP wire, where every word cost money—and never
unlearned it. Declarative sentences. Short words. No adverbs. No flowery language.

**Signature principle (Iceberg Theory):** "There is seven-eighths of it underwater for every
part that shows. Anything you know you can eliminate and it will strengthen the story." Cut
what you know. The reader will feel it anyway.

The prose effect: the reader feels the emotion without being told to feel it. "Isn't it pretty
to think so?" Seven words. The last line of *The Sun Also Rises*. Contains the entire novel's
grief.

---

### Joan Didion (1934–2021)
*Slouching Towards Bethlehem*, *The White Album*, *The Year of Magical Thinking*

The rhythmist. Didion's sentences move with the precision of a metronome that occasionally
breaks. She uses fragmentation and repetition—incantatory repetition—to reproduce the texture
of thought under pressure. Her sentences are short or long, never medium-length, because medium
is comfortable and comfort is the enemy of perception.

**Signature principle:** "We tell ourselves stories in order to live." Prose should reveal
what narrative is doing to us, not just what it says. The syntax performs the meaning.

"I write entirely to find out what I'm thinking, what I'm looking at, what I see and what it
means."

---

### John McPhee (b. 1931)
*Annals of the Former World*, *Coming into the Country*, *Draft No. 4* (2017)

The structuralist. McPhee is the greatest living practitioner of long-form nonfiction. He
taught at Princeton for decades; his students include David Remnick and Richard Preston. His
genius is structural: he treats the architecture of a piece as a narrative device in itself—
invisible to the reader, essential to the experience.

**Signature principles:**
- "A piece of writing has to start somewhere, go somewhere, and sit down when it gets there."
- "Readers are not supposed to notice the structure."
- "A thousand details add up to one impression."
- *Greening*: remove words so cleanly that no one notices anything was removed.

McPhee also coined the "double lead"—beginning with two separate scenes that resonate against
each other so that "one plus one adds up to more than two."

---

### Tracy Kidder (b. 1945)
*The Soul of a New Machine* (1981, Pulitzer Prize), *Mountains Beyond Mountains*

The humanizer. Kidder proved that technical subjects—chip design, a doctor's work in Haiti—
become page-turning literature when you put human beings at the center and get the technical
details exactly right. His prose is urgent without being breathless, precise without being
cold.

**Signature principle:** Journalistic precision plus literary structure equals nonfiction that
reads like a novel. Embed the technical in the human. Get the details right—wrong details
break the spell completely.

---

### Annie Dillard (b. 1945)
*Pilgrim at Tinker Creek* (1974, Pulitzer Prize), *The Writing Life*

The witness. Dillard makes intense close observation her method and her subject. She trained
herself to notice—to really see what is in front of her, not the category ("bird") but the
specific thing ("a merganser, female, with twelve ducklings in tow, the last three struggling
at the edge of the current"). Then she builds outward from that specific observation to the
widest possible question.

**Signature principle:** Start with the concrete and particular. Everything abstract and
universal must be earned by a specific observation first. "In the particular is contained the
universal."

---

### Steven Pinker (b. 1954)
*The Sense of Style* (2014)

The cognitive scientist's answer to Strunk and White. Pinker grounds style advice in how the
mind actually reads. His central contribution: *classic style*. Not the stiff, hedged,
defensive writing of academic papers—classic style where the writer has seen something and
guides the reader to see it too.

**Signature principle:** "The guiding metaphor of classic style is seeing the world. The
writer can see something that the reader has not yet noticed, and orients the reader's gaze
so that she can see it for herself." Write as if you're pointing at a thing in the world, not
managing the reader's expectations about what you might possibly mean under certain
interpretations.

Pinker is also excellent on the curse of knowledge: writers forget that the reader doesn't
know what they know, leading to opaque prose that skips necessary steps.

---

### Verlyn Klinkenborg (b. 1952)
*Several Short Sentences About Writing* (2012), NYT editorial board for twenty years

The radical. Klinkenborg rejects almost all conventional writing instruction. His method:
make each sentence work. Not paragraphs, not essays—sentences. Read every sentence alone. If
it can't stand alone, it's doing something wrong. Remove the scaffolding.

**Signature principle:** "Your job as a writer is making sentences. Your other jobs—fixing
sentences, killing sentences, arranging sentences—are extensions of that fundamental job."

Complexity is not intelligence. Remove words instead of adding them. A short clear sentence is
harder to write than a long muddy one.

---

### Paul Graham (b. 1964)
paulgraham.com essays (2001–present)

The technologist-essayist. Graham writes about startups, ideas, education, and thinking. His
prose is conversational, direct, and structurally surprising—deceptively simple sentences that
move fast and land hard. He explicitly advocates writing the way you talk.

**Signature principles:**
- "The less energy readers expend on your prose, the more they'll have for your ideas."
- "Write in spoken language. Don't use words you wouldn't use in speech."
- "Writing simply keeps you honest. If you say nothing simply, it will be obvious to everyone,
  including you."
- Process: write fast, edit slow. "Expect 80% of the ideas in an essay to happen after you
  start writing it."

Graham's essays are models of the exploratory form—they begin with a question and actually
answer it, rather than gesturing toward answering it.

---

### The New Yorker (institution, 1925–present)
Key writers: James Baldwin, Janet Malcolm, John McPhee, Elizabeth Kolbert, Elif Batuman,
Adam Gopnik, David Remnick

The institutional standard. The New Yorker's house principle was articulated by a former staff
writer as: "Clarity, harmony, truth, and unfailing courtesy to the reader." Meticulous
fact-checking is built into the process—accuracy and clarity are treated as the same virtue.

**Signature principle:** Every sentence must carry its freight. The magazine publishes long
pieces, but length is never filler—it's structure. Each sentence moves the reader forward.

---

### The Economist (institution, 1843–present)
*The Economist Style Guide* (multiple editions)

The newspaper of record for compressed, elegant argumentation. The *Economist* refuses hedging,
refuses jargon, and insists on specificity. Its writers are anonymous—there is no byline—which
enforces a collective discipline: the prose must carry the argument, not the author's
reputation.

**Signature principle:** "Think what you want to say, then say it as simply as possible."
Reproduce Orwell's six rules. Prefer active voice, short sentences, concrete nouns. Never use
a long word when a short one does the job.

---

### Richard Feynman (1918–1988)
*The Feynman Lectures on Physics*, *Surely You're Joking, Mr. Feynman!*, *Six Easy Pieces*

The explainer. Feynman won the Nobel Prize in Physics and was known as "The Great Explainer"
because he refused to hide behind technical language. He insisted that if you couldn't explain
something in plain English, you didn't understand it yet.

**Signature principle:** Build from first principles, in plain words, step by step. If you
reach for jargon before you've laid the foundation, you've lost the reader—and possibly
yourself. Test your explanation on a complete novice. If they're confused, the explanation is
wrong.

---

### Christopher Hitchens (1949–2011)
*Letters to a Young Contrarian*, *Mortality*, *god Is Not Great*

The polemicist. Hitchens wrote with moral urgency and intellectual precision. His sentences
are balanced, his vocabulary exact, his arguments direct. He invokes Orwell constantly—not
as decoration but as practice.

**Signature principle:** Clarity is a moral stance. Fuzzy prose conceals fuzzy (or dishonest)
thinking. The writer who hedges on every claim isn't being careful—they're being cowardly.
State the thing plainly and stand behind it.

---

### Mary Oliver (1935–2019)
*Upstream: Selected Essays* (2016), *Blue Pastures* (1995)

The attention practitioner. Oliver's poetry is better known than her prose, but her essays
demonstrate the same principle in longer form: pay total attention to the actual thing in front
of you. "Attention is the beginning of devotion." Not the category of the thing. The thing.

**Signature principle:** The particular precedes the general. Observe first, conclude second.
Never reverse the order. "Instructions for living a life: Pay attention. Be astonished. Tell
about it."

## 2. CHECKABLE ELITENESS CRITERIA

These are objective, auditable properties. Not matters of taste—gates.

### TIER 1 — HARD FAILURES (reject immediately)

**A. AI-slop vocabulary (any of these → fail)**

Single-word slop (direct substitution reveals the problem):
- `delve` → dig into, examine, look at
- `utilize` → use
- `leverage` (as a verb) → use, apply, take advantage of
- `facilitate` → help, enable, allow
- `elucidate` → explain, show, clarify
- `embark` → start, begin
- `endeavor` → try, work, effort
- `encompass` → include, cover
- `multifaceted` → complex, varied, several-sided
- `tapestry` → describe the actual thing
- `testament` → shows, proves, demonstrates
- `paradigm` → model, approach, system
- `synergy` / `synergize` → delete and restart
- `holistic` → whole, complete
- `catalyze` / `catalyst` (metaphorical) → cause, trigger, spark
- `juxtapose` → compare, contrast, place beside
- `nuanced` (as filler) → delete entirely or say what the nuance is
- `realm` → area, field, space
- `landscape` (metaphorical) → field, situation, terrain
- `myriad` → many
- `plethora` → many, a lot
- `impactful` → powerful, effective, significant
- `robust` (as vague modifier) → strong, reliable, thorough — or cut

**B. Filler phrases (delete, no replacement needed)**
- "It's worth noting that"
- "It's important to note that"
- "Importantly," / "Notably," / "Interestingly,"
- "Let's dive into" / "Let's explore"
- "In this section, we will"
- "As we can see" / "As mentioned earlier"
- "In conclusion" / "To summarize"
- "Furthermore," / "Moreover," / "Additionally," (as paragraph openers)
- "In today's [fast-paced / digital / modern] world"
- "At the end of the day"
- "It goes without saying"
- "Without further ado"
- "When it comes to"
- "In the realm of"
- "One might argue that" / "It could be suggested that"
- "This begs the question"
- "Not just X, but Y" (used formulaically)

**C. Opening-sentence failures**
Any piece that opens with:
- A definition ("X is defined as...")
- A rhetorical question ("Have you ever wondered...?")
- A historical sweep ("Throughout history, humans have...")
- A statistics-as-throat-clearing sentence ("According to a 2023 study...")
- Direct reader address ("In today's fast-paced world...")

### TIER 2 — STRUCTURAL FAILURES (require revision)

**D. Passive voice overuse**
More than 1 passive per 5 sentences is a warning sign. More than 2 per 5 is a failure. The
test: can you add "by zombies" to the end of the sentence? If yes, it's passive.

**E. Hedge stacking**
Legitimate hedging is "the data suggest" or "probably." Hedge stacking is "it may perhaps
be possible that in some cases this could potentially..." Count hedges per sentence. More than
one hedge per sentence without strong justification → revise.

**F. Abstract-without-anchor**
Any sentence making a general claim must be followed (within 2-3 sentences) by a specific
example, number, or concrete detail. "Many organizations face this challenge" → what
organizations? Which challenge? Pinker calls this the curse of knowledge in reverse: the
writer has no specific case in mind, and the prose floats.

**G. Adverb bloat**
-ly adverbs modifying weak verbs signal that the wrong verb was chosen. "He walked quickly"
→ "He strode." "She spoke softly" → "She murmured." Adverbs are not forbidden—they are
emergency instruments. Overuse reveals under-editing.

### TIER 3 — RHYTHM AND REPETITION FAILURES (quality signal)

**H. Sentence-length monotony**
Elite prose varies sentence length deliberately. Didion alternates long and short; McPhee
uses long sentences with internal structure; Hemingway uses short sentences that accumulate.
Three or more sentences of near-identical length in a row → check if this is creating rhythm
(good) or monotony (bad).

**I. Lexical repetition (degeneration signal)**
The same non-function word appearing more than twice in a 150-word passage without deliberate
rhetorical intent (anaphora, etc.) → the model is looping or lacking range. Count and flag.

**J. Suspiciously balanced structure**
"There are three reasons. First... Second... Third..." is the model falling back to a template.
Elite prose sometimes has three reasons, but they're found in the argument, not imposed on it.
Count numbered lists masquerading as prose.

### GOLD 1: Technical explanation — how a transformer attention head works

**Prompt:** Explain what a transformer attention head is doing, concretely, so a software
engineer with no ML background can follow.

**Elite response:**

Think of a sentence as a row of people at a dinner party. Each person has something to say,
but before they speak, they look around and decide whose words matter most to what they want
to express. That looking-around is attention.

In a transformer, every word in a sequence learns to do this. It computes three things:
a query (what it's looking for), a key (what it advertises about itself), and a value (what
it actually contributes once chosen). The query of word A is compared against the keys of
every other word. Where the match is strong, word A borrows a large portion of that word's
value. Where the match is weak, it borrows almost nothing.

The result is a new representation of word A—one that has absorbed context from wherever in
the sentence the match was high. "Bank" near "river" pulls in river-bank context. "Bank" near
"mortgage" pulls in finance-bank context. The word looks the same on the surface; underneath,
it has changed.

A single attention head does this for one kind of relationship. Transformers run eight or
sixteen heads in parallel, each trained to find a different kind of match—grammatical role,
semantic field, coreference, position. Their outputs are concatenated, then projected back
down to a single vector. That vector is what gets passed to the next layer.

The expensive part is the quadratic scaling: every word must attend to every other word, so
a sequence of length N requires N-squared operations per head. This is why long-context models
are costly to run and why hardware engineers spend so much time trying to make attention
faster.

---

### GOLD 2: Technical explanation — why quantization hurts some models more than others

**Prompt:** Why does quantizing a large language model to 4-bit sometimes destroy its
performance while other models survive it fine?

**Elite response:**

A model's weights are floating-point numbers—each one a position on a number line. When you
quantize, you round each weight to the nearest point on a coarser grid. 4-bit gives you 16
possible values instead of thousands. The question is whether your weights are spread across
that range uniformly, or whether most of them cluster in a narrow band with a few large
outliers.

If the distribution is clean—roughly bell-shaped, no wild outliers—then 16 bins capture it
reasonably well. The rounding error is small and spread evenly. The model barely notices.

But some models accumulate large weights in specific positions. Attention output projections
and certain MLP layers in deep models can produce weights 10x or 100x larger than the median.
Quantizing to a single scale factor (one number shared across 32 or 64 weights) means the
scale is set by the outlier, which crushes the resolution for everything else. The result:
most weights get rounded to zero or near-zero, destroying the information they carried.

The fix is either to quantize those layers at higher precision (leaving them in 8-bit while
quantizing the rest to 4-bit), or to use a scheme like GPTQ or AWQ that solves for the best
quantization grid given the model's actual weight distribution. AWQ, for instance, identifies
the few weights that matter most—those the model's activations amplify—and protects them.

Models trained with weight regularization or explicit noise during training tend to survive
quantization better because the training process has already discouraged outlier weights. It
is not magic; it is that the pre-quantized weights are already in a friendlier distribution.

### GOLD 3: Essay — what makes writing hard

**Prompt:** What makes writing hard? Not mechanics—the actual difficulty.

**Elite response:**

The hard part is not sentences. You can learn sentences. The hard part is that writing
forces you to find out whether you know what you think you know.

You have an idea. It feels solid. You sit down to write it and it dissolves. What looked like
a thought was a feeling about a thought—a vague sense that something was true, held together
by the fact that you'd never had to state it plainly. The moment you commit to a sentence,
the thought either survives the test or it doesn't.

This is why most people avoid writing, even when they intend to write. They stay in the
planning phase. They make outlines. They do more research. The outline feels like progress;
it keeps the thought in its comfortable unexamined state. Writing destroys this comfort.

The second difficulty is caring without showing it. If you care too little, the prose is
lifeless and the reader feels it. If you care too much, the prose becomes anxious and
performative—you start managing the reader's reaction rather than giving them the thing. The
tone has to be like the tone of someone pointing at something in the distance: interested,
alert, not desperate for you to see it their way.

The third is that sentences don't arrive complete. The first draft is usually a record of
your confusion. The material is there but scrambled. Rewriting is not fixing typos—it's
finding the sentence that was hiding inside the wrong sentence you wrote first. You cut toward
clarity the way a sculptor cuts toward the figure.

Most writers feel like frauds for this reason. They assume real writers generate polished
prose. They don't. They generate drafts.

### GOLD 4: Technical documentation — explaining a function's behavior

**Prompt:** Document the behavior of `mx.set_memory_limit(limit_bytes)` in MLX for a
developer who needs to understand when and why to use it.

**Elite response:**

`mx.set_memory_limit(limit_bytes)` tells MLX's allocator to stop requesting new memory from
the OS once the active allocation reaches `limit_bytes`. Beyond that threshold, any allocation
that would exceed the limit triggers an OOM error rather than growing the working set further.

**When you need it**

On Apple Silicon, the GPU and CPU share the same physical RAM. When a model and its KV cache
expand without bound, they can quietly exhaust the pool that the OS also needs for system
processes. The failure mode is a crash that arrives without warning—sometimes minutes into
inference, sometimes hours in, depending on context length. Setting a limit converts the
silent crash into a catchable exception, which you can then handle by clearing the cache,
reducing batch size, or aborting gracefully.

**What it is not**

It does not reduce the memory a model has already allocated. If your model weights occupy
90 GB and you set a limit of 96 GB, you have 6 GB for activations and KV cache combined.
The limit is a ceiling on further growth, not a compaction of existing state.

**Typical usage pattern**

```python
import mlx.core as mx

# Reserve 8 GB headroom for OS + system processes on a 128 GB machine
mx.set_memory_limit(120 * 1024 ** 3)

try:
    run_inference(prompt)
except mx.core.MemoryError:
    mx.metal.clear_cache()
    run_inference(prompt, max_tokens=256)  # retry with reduced output length
```

**Choosing the value**

Set it 5-10 GB below total physical RAM. The OS needs roughly 6-8 GB for normal operation on
macOS; leave more headroom if you're running other memory-intensive processes. Watch `htop` or
Activity Monitor's memory pressure graph during a first inference run to calibrate.

### GOLD 5: Explanatory essay — what attention to detail does in good writing

**Prompt:** Why does specific detail make prose better? What's the mechanism?

**Elite response:**

The claim is abstract. The detail is concrete. The reader's brain processes concrete things
faster and remembers them longer. This is not an aesthetic preference—it is how memory works.

When you write "he was nervous," the reader files it as information and moves on. When you
write "he tore the corner off the napkin and rolled it between his fingers," the reader sees
it. They have performed a small act of imagination, which means they have done some of the
work, which means they feel the nervous energy rather than being told about it. The difference
is the difference between being told a joke and getting a joke.

Specific detail also functions as proof. "The company faced challenges" is a claim that
demands trust; "the company burned through three CFOs in eighteen months" earns trust. The
specific fact signals that someone has actually looked at the thing. Vague language suggests
the opposite.

There is a third function, less discussed. Specific detail fixes the reader in time and place.
"It was a cold morning" is a stage direction. "The car took four turns of the key to start
and the steering wheel was too cold to hold with bare hands" puts the reader in a body, in
December, in a parking lot. The reader is no longer floating above the prose—they're in it.

Didion knew this. McPhee built a career on it. Oliver took it to its extreme: the detail is
not evidence of a point, the detail *is* the point. When she writes "a snapping turtle, the
size of a dinner plate," she is not building toward a claim about snapping turtles. She is
giving you the turtle. That is the whole essay's purpose.

The failure mode of abstract prose is that it never arrives. It makes gestures toward meaning
without providing the thing that would produce meaning in the reader. Detail is not decoration.
It is the mechanism.

## 4. ELITENESS AUDIT — PYTHON PSEUDOCODE

A gating function that scores a candidate text. Returns pass/fail + diagnostics.

```python
import re
from collections import Counter

# ── TIER 1: HARD FAILURES ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────

SLOP_TIER1 = {
    "delve", "utilize", "elucidate", "embark", "endeavor", "encompass",
    "multifaceted", "tapestry", "testament", "paradigm", "synergy", "synergize",
    "holistic", "catalyze", "catalyst", "juxtapose", "nuanced", "realm",
    "myriad", "plethora", "impactful", "leverage",  # as verb — checked in context
    "facilitate",
}

SLOP_TIER2_PHRASES = [
    "it's worth noting", "it's important to note", "let's dive into",
    "let's explore", "as we can see", "as mentioned earlier",
    "in conclusion", "to summarize", "furthermore,", "moreover,",
    "additionally,", "in today's", "at the end of the day",
    "it goes without saying", "without further ado", "when it comes to",
    "in the realm of", "one might argue", "it could be suggested",
    "this begs the question", "not just", "notably,", "interestingly,",
    "importantly,", "in this section",
]

BAD_OPENERS = [
    r"^[A-Z][a-z]+ is defined as",
    r"^Have you ever",
    r"^Throughout history",
    r"^According to a \d{4}",
    r"^In today's",
    r"^[A-Z][a-zA-Z ]+, \d{4}–?\d{0,4}\.",  # biographical bracket opening
]


def check_tier1(text: str) -> list[str]:
    """Return list of hard-fail slop words found."""
    words = re.findall(r"\b\w+\b", text.lower())
    hits = [w for w in words if w in SLOP_TIER1]
    return list(set(hits))


def check_tier2_phrases(text: str) -> list[str]:
    """Return list of filler phrases found."""
    low = text.lower()
    return [p for p in SLOP_TIER2_PHRASES if p in low]


def check_bad_opener(text: str) -> bool:
    """True if opening sentence matches a known bad pattern."""
    first_sentence = text.split(".")[0].strip()
    return any(re.match(pat, first_sentence) for pat in BAD_OPENERS)


# ── TIER 2: STRUCTURAL FAILURES ───────────────────────────────────────────────

def passive_voice_ratio(text: str) -> float:
    """
    Heuristic: count 'was/were/is/are/been + past participle' constructions.
    A proper parse would use spaCy dep tags; this is a proxy.
    Returns fraction of sentences that appear passive.
    """
    sentences = [s.strip() for s in re.split(r"[.!?]", text) if s.strip()]
    passive_pattern = re.compile(
        r"\b(was|were|is|are|been|be)\b\s+\w+ed\b", re.IGNORECASE
    )
    passive_count = sum(1 for s in sentences if passive_pattern.search(s))
    return passive_count / max(len(sentences), 1)


def hedge_density(text: str) -> float:
    """Count hedge words per sentence. > 0.6 hedges/sentence → warn."""
    hedges = re.findall(
        r"\b(maybe|perhaps|possibly|potentially|might|may|could|seem|appear|suggest"
        r"|indicate|tend|often|sometimes|generally|typically|usually|arguably)\b",
        text, re.IGNORECASE
    )
    sentences = [s for s in re.split(r"[.!?]", text) if s.strip()]
    return len(hedges) / max(len(sentences), 1)


def adverb_density(text: str) -> float:
    """Count -ly adverbs. > 1 per 50 words is excessive."""
    adverbs = re.findall(r"\b\w+ly\b", text, re.IGNORECASE)
    words = text.split()
    return len(adverbs) / max(len(words), 1) * 50  # adverbs per 50 words


# ── TIER 3: RHYTHM AND REPETITION ─────────────────────────────────────────────

def sentence_length_variance(text: str) -> float:
    """
    Elite prose varies sentence length. Low variance over 5+ sentences suggests
    template-generated monotony. Returns standard deviation of word counts.
    """
    sentences = [s.strip() for s in re.split(r"[.!?]", text) if len(s.strip()) > 10]
    if len(sentences) < 3:
        return 0.0
    lengths = [len(s.split()) for s in sentences]
    mean = sum(lengths) / len(lengths)
    variance = sum((l - mean) ** 2 for l in lengths) / len(lengths)
    return variance ** 0.5  # std dev; below 5.0 on a 500-word passage → warn


def lexical_repetition(text: str, window: int = 150) -> list[tuple[str, int]]:
    """
    Flag non-function words that appear 3+ times in any 150-word window.
    Degeneration signal: the model is looping.
    """
    STOP = {
        "the", "a", "an", "and", "or", "but", "in", "on", "at", "to", "for",
        "of", "with", "by", "from", "as", "is", "was", "are", "were", "be",
        "been", "this", "that", "it", "he", "she", "they", "we", "you", "i",
        "not", "no", "so", "if", "then", "than", "more", "also", "have", "has",
        "had", "will", "would", "can", "could", "do", "did", "does", "their",
        "there", "what", "which", "who", "when", "how", "its", "his", "her",
    }
    words = re.findall(r"\b[a-z]+\b", text.lower())
    hits = []
    for start in range(0, len(words), window // 2):
        chunk = words[start : start + window]
        freq = Counter(w for w in chunk if w not in STOP and len(w) > 3)
        hits.extend([(w, c) for w, c in freq.items() if c >= 3])
    return list(set(hits))


def numbered_list_fraction(text: str) -> float:
    """
    Elite prose doesn't lean on numbered lists. Fraction of sentences that are
    list items (start with a digit + period/paren). > 0.25 → warn.
    """
    sentences = [s.strip() for s in text.split("\n") if s.strip()]
    list_items = sum(1 for s in sentences if re.match(r"^\d+[.)]\s", s))
    return list_items / max(len(sentences), 1)


# ── MASTER GATE ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

def audit_elite(text: str, verbose: bool = True) -> dict:
    """
    Returns a result dict. 'pass' is True only if all hard gates clear and
    soft metrics are within tolerance.
    """
    result = {"pass": True, "failures": [], "warnings": []}

    # Hard failures
    slop = check_tier1(text)
    if slop:
        result["pass"] = False
        result["failures"].append(f"SLOP_TIER1: {slop}")

    phrases = check_tier2_phrases(text)
    if phrases:
        result["pass"] = False
        result["failures"].append(f"FILLER_PHRASES: {phrases}")

    if check_bad_opener(text):
        result["pass"] = False
        result["failures"].append("BAD_OPENER")

    # Soft warnings
    pv = passive_voice_ratio(text)
    if pv > 0.35:
        result["warnings"].append(f"PASSIVE_RATIO: {pv:.2f} (>0.35 warn, >0.5 fail)")
    if pv > 0.5:
        result["pass"] = False
        result["failures"].append(f"PASSIVE_OVERUSE: {pv:.2f}")

    hd = hedge_density(text)
    if hd > 0.6:
        result["warnings"].append(f"HEDGE_DENSITY: {hd:.2f} hedges/sentence")
    if hd > 1.2:
        result["pass"] = False
        result["failures"].append(f"HEDGE_STACK: {hd:.2f}")

    ad = adverb_density(text)
    if ad > 1.5:
        result["warnings"].append(f"ADVERB_DENSITY: {ad:.2f} per 50 words")

    slv = sentence_length_variance(text)
    if slv < 4.0 and len(text.split()) > 150:
        result["warnings"].append(f"MONOTONE_RHYTHM: std_dev={slv:.1f} (low variety)")

    reps = lexical_repetition(text)
    if reps:
        result["warnings"].append(f"REPETITION: {reps[:5]}")
        if len(reps) > 5:
            result["pass"] = False
            result["failures"].append(f"DEGENERATION: {len(reps)} repeated terms")

    nlf = numbered_list_fraction(text)
    if nlf > 0.25:
        result["warnings"].append(f"LIST_HEAVY: {nlf:.0%} sentences are list items")

    if verbose:
        status = "PASS" if result["pass"] else "FAIL"
        print(f"[{status}] failures={result['failures']} warnings={result['warnings']}")

    return result
```

## 5. HOW TO USE THIS FOR SFT DATA GENERATION

### The generation pipeline

1. **Prompt design.** Prompts should be genuine tasks: explain a concept, write a short
   essay, document a function, answer a hard question. Avoid meta-prompts ("write an essay
   about writing"). The response should be something a skilled human writer would actually
   produce.

2. **Candidate generation.** Generate 5-10 responses per prompt from the best available
   model. Do not filter by length; filter by quality.

3. **Automated gate.** Run `audit_elite()` on every candidate. Reject hard failures
   immediately. Flag warnings for human review.

4. **Human review (the non-negotiable step).** Every passing candidate gets human eyes.
   Ask: Would McPhee be embarrassed by this? Would Orwell cut it further? Is every sentence
   doing work?

5. **SFT format.** Paired examples: `{"prompt": ..., "response": ...}`. The response is the
   elite prose version. The rejected candidates can optionally be used as negative examples
   in DPO/RLAIF.

### What to prioritize

Technical explanations that are vivid and specific outperform generic "here is an overview"
responses by the largest margin. The model can already produce correct explanations; what
it lacks is the specificity, the concrete example, the sentence that lands.

Short essays (300-600 words) on hard questions demonstrate the exploratory form. They should
begin with a surprise, develop honestly, and end when the thought is complete—not when a
template demands a conclusion.

Documentation pieces should show that technical precision and readable prose are not
opposites. The MLX example above (Gold 4) is a model: specific API, clear "what it is not"
section, code, and a calibration rule.

### The one test

After writing or reviewing a candidate, apply Mary Oliver's test: is there a single image,
fact, or sentence in here that a reader will remember tomorrow? If not, the piece needs more
specific detail.
